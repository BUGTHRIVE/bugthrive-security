# Rules of Engagement

These Rules of Engagement define the requirements for conducting security research on BugThrive assets authorized under the BugThrive Vulnerability Disclosure Policy.

By testing BugThrive assets, you agree to follow these rules, the [Security Policy](SECURITY.md), the [Authorized Scope](SCOPE.md), and all applicable laws.

---

## Research Principles

Security research must be conducted:

- In good faith
- Responsibly and ethically
- Only against authorized, in-scope assets
- Using the least-invasive method reasonably available
- Without harming BugThrive, its users, customers, employees, systems, services, or data
- Without disrupting the availability, reliability, or performance of BugThrive services
- With the sole purpose of identifying and responsibly reporting security vulnerabilities

Testing must stop when sufficient evidence has been collected to demonstrate the existence and potential impact of a vulnerability.

Do not continue exploiting a vulnerability after it has been reasonably demonstrated.

---

## Authorized Testing

Researchers may:

- Test officially deployed and actively operated BugThrive assets listed in the [Authorized Scope](SCOPE.md)
- Perform manual security testing using non-destructive techniques
- Create and use accounts owned and controlled by the researcher
- Test authorization boundaries only between accounts owned and controlled by the researcher
- Inspect publicly accessible application behavior, requests, responses, APIs, and client-side resources
- Use automated security tools when they are carefully configured, rate-limited, non-disruptive, and followed by manual validation
- Create a limited proof of concept when necessary to demonstrate a vulnerability safely
- Submit findings through GitHub Private Vulnerability Reporting or by email to `compliance@bugthrive.com`

Authorization applies only to assets explicitly covered by BugThrive’s published scope.

---

## Account and Authorization Testing

When testing authentication, authorization, account separation, or privilege boundaries:

- Use only accounts that you own and control
- Do not access another user’s account without explicit authorization
- Do not attempt to obtain or use another user’s credentials
- Do not modify another user’s profile, permissions, content, settings, or data
- Do not perform actions on behalf of another user
- Do not access additional records solely to demonstrate broader impact
- Use the minimum access necessary to validate the vulnerability

If testing requires multiple user roles, use accounts that you are authorized to create and control.

---

## Automated Testing

Automated scanners and security testing tools are permitted only when their use is:

- Limited to in-scope assets
- Carefully configured
- Rate-limited
- Non-destructive
- Non-disruptive
- Unlikely to affect service availability or performance
- Unlikely to generate excessive traffic, requests, accounts, records, notifications, emails, or logs

All automated findings must be manually reviewed and validated before submission.

Do not submit:

- Raw scanner output without validation
- Unverified findings
- Tool-generated reports without clear reproduction steps
- Duplicate findings generated by repeated scans
- Findings based only on software version detection
- Findings without demonstrated security impact
- Large volumes of low-quality or informational results

Reports consisting primarily of unverified automated output may be closed without further explanation.

Permission to use automated tools does not authorize denial-of-service testing, stress testing, load testing, aggressive scanning, resource exhaustion, or excessive traffic generation.

---

## Prohibited Activities

The following activities are prohibited:

- Denial-of-service or distributed denial-of-service testing
- Stress testing, load testing, or resource-exhaustion testing
- Intentionally degrading service availability, reliability, or performance
- Excessive, aggressive, or disruptive automated scanning
- Brute-force attacks
- Password spraying
- Credential stuffing
- Testing credentials obtained from breaches, credential dumps, malware, leaks, or unauthorized sources
- Social engineering
- Phishing
- Pretexting
- Impersonating BugThrive employees, contractors, customers, users, vendors, or partners
- Contacting BugThrive employees or users for testing purposes without authorization
- Physical attacks against offices, personnel, infrastructure, equipment, or facilities
- Spam or unsolicited communications
- Malware, ransomware, spyware, destructive payloads, or persistent malicious code
- Establishing persistence in BugThrive systems
- Installing backdoors or creating unauthorized access mechanisms
- Privilege escalation beyond what is minimally necessary to demonstrate the vulnerability
- Lateral movement between systems, services, accounts, or environments
- Modifying, deleting, corrupting, encrypting, or destroying data
- Accessing, copying, downloading, retaining, transferring, selling, or publicly disclosing sensitive information
- Accessing another user’s account or data
- Modifying another user’s information, permissions, content, or settings
- Testing customer-owned systems or environments
- Testing third-party applications, integrations, vendors, infrastructure, or services without authorization
- Testing unofficial, deprecated, unsupported, unauthorized, or “Coming Soon” deployments
- Exploiting a vulnerability for personal, financial, commercial, competitive, or malicious advantage
- Extortion, threats, coercion, or demands related to vulnerability disclosure or remediation
- Public disclosure without prior confirmation from BugThrive
- Any activity prohibited by applicable law

---

## Sensitive Information

If you encounter personal information, customer information, credentials, passwords, authentication tokens, API keys, secrets, financial information, confidential information, proprietary information, or other sensitive data:

1. Stop testing immediately.
2. Do not continue accessing or exploring the information.
3. Do not access additional records to demonstrate impact.
4. Do not copy, download, retain, modify, delete, transfer, share, or publicly disclose the information.
5. Report the issue to BugThrive immediately.
6. Include only the minimum evidence necessary to explain and validate the vulnerability.
7. Redact sensitive information from screenshots, videos, proofs of concept, and reports wherever possible.

If sensitive information is unintentionally retained, securely delete it after BugThrive confirms that it is no longer required for investigation.

---

## Proof-of-Concept Requirements

Proofs of concept should be:

- Limited to the minimum actions necessary to demonstrate the vulnerability
- Non-destructive
- Performed using researcher-owned accounts and data whenever possible
- Designed to avoid affecting other users
- Free from unnecessary sensitive information
- Shared privately through an approved reporting channel

Do not use a proof of concept to establish persistence, access unrelated systems, extract unnecessary data, or demonstrate impact beyond what is reasonably required.

---

## Reporting Requirements

Reports should include:

- Vulnerability title
- Short description
- Affected asset
- Clear steps to reproduce
- Proof of concept, when available and safe
- Potential security impact
- Screenshots or videos, when applicable
- Researcher’s full name
- Researcher’s email address

Suggested remediation is optional.

If screenshots, videos, or a proof of concept are not included, the reproduction steps must be sufficiently detailed for BugThrive to independently reproduce and assess the finding.

---

## Approved Reporting Channels

The recommended reporting channel is GitHub Private Vulnerability Reporting.

Reports may also be submitted to:

`compliance@bugthrive.com`

Do not disclose vulnerability information through:

- Public GitHub Issues
- GitHub Discussions
- Pull requests
- Public commits
- Social media
- Blogs
- Forums
- Public advisories
- Videos
- Conferences
- Mailing lists
- News publications
- Other public channels

---

## Communication and Cooperation

Researchers are expected to:

- Communicate accurately and professionally
- Respond to reasonable requests for clarification
- Provide additional information when necessary
- Avoid misleading, exaggerated, or unsupported claims
- Keep vulnerability information confidential
- Allow BugThrive reasonable time to investigate and remediate the issue
- Coordinate disclosure with BugThrive
- Inform BugThrive before conducting testing that may create uncertainty or unintended risk

BugThrive may close reports when the researcher does not provide information reasonably required to reproduce or assess the finding.

---

## Coordinated Disclosure

Vulnerability information must remain confidential until BugThrive confirms that public disclosure may proceed.

Submission, acknowledgement, validation, remediation, or closure of a report does not automatically grant permission for public disclosure.

Researchers must receive confirmation from BugThrive before publicly disclosing:

- Vulnerability details
- Technical findings
- Proofs of concept
- Screenshots or videos
- Exploitation methods
- Remediation details
- Private communications
- Investigation information

---

## Policy Violations

Failure to follow these Rules of Engagement may result in:

- Closure of the report
- Removal from the disclosure process
- Ineligibility for recognition
- Restriction from future participation
- Loss of safe-harbor consideration
- Other appropriate action when necessary

Safe-harbor protections apply only to good-faith research conducted in accordance with the published BugThrive policies.

---

## Questions Before Testing

If you are uncertain whether an activity or asset is authorized, contact:

`compliance@bugthrive.com`

Request clarification before proceeding. Do not assume authorization.
